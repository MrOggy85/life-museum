I think there are actually two different ideas here. The second is stronger.

Idea 1: Delayed camera

A web app that:

* Opens directly to the camera.
* Capture one photo or a 2–5 second clip.
* Uploads immediately.
* Doesn’t let you browse today’s captures.
* Tomorrow (or next week), they become available.

This changes behavior because it removes the urge to immediately inspect every shot.

The implementation is straightforward:

* Camera API (getUserMedia)
* Upload to server
* Store EXIF + location + timestamp
* Hide media until a configurable delay expires

Idea 2: AI memory journal

This is the more interesting part.

Every capture becomes a memory entry, not just a file.

For example:

July 2, 2026

📍 Kichijoji

🕐 18:14

🌤 29°C

“You stopped for coffee after work. This was your third visit to this café this month.”

Notice that none of this has to come from image recognition.

You already know:

* timestamp
* GPS
* weather
* movement speed
* duration at location
* previous visits

Image understanding can add things like:

“Looks like ramen.”

“Indoor café.”

“Two people.”

“Cherry blossoms.”

Then an LLM combines everything into a journal entry.

Even better: AI notices patterns

Instead of describing one image, summarize many.

For example:

This week you spent more time outside than usual.

You visited three new places.

You haven’t taken any evening walks this week.

You met Alex twice.

This becomes much more valuable than image captions.

Why video is better

I actually think Setlog got something right.

A two-second moving clip contains:

* voices
* ambient sounds
* gestures
* movement

Years later, a tiny video is often far more evocative than a still.

Storage isn’t a huge issue.

For example:

* 2-second H.264 clip ≈ 1–3 MB
* 10 clips/day ≈ 20 MB
* One year ≈ 7 GB

That’s manageable.

I would avoid GIF entirely.

GIF:

* huge file sizes
* no audio
* poor colors
* outdated format

Instead:

* MP4 (H.264)
* or HEVC if supported

Generate a GIF or thumbnail only for previews if needed.

One thing I would steal from film cameras

Don’t let users delete clips immediately.

Imagine:

* Record.
* Upload.
* Gone.

No retakes.

No reviewing.

Tomorrow you see what happened.

That encourages documenting life instead of curating it.

AI features I’d actually use

Rather than flashy computer vision, I’d want:

* “Show me every rainy day.”
* “Every coffee shop I visited.”
* “Every Saturday with friends.”
* “My commute changed here.”
* “When did I first visit this place?”
* “Generate a monthly recap.”
* “What was I doing around this time last year?”

These leverage accumulated context over time.

A small twist

Instead of thinking of it as a camera app, think of it as a memory capture system.

The media is just one input.

Each entry could contain:

* 2-second video
* Location
* Weather
* Motion state (walking, driving, stationary)
* Calendar event (optional)
* AI summary
* Similar memories from previous years

Over months and years, that becomes a personal timeline rather than a gallery. The value grows with time, which is a property many apps lack.

This also aligns with your concern about privacy. A self-hosted system means the data remains under your control while still allowing AI enrichment. If you keep the raw media on your own server and use local models when practical, you can get many of the benefits without handing your life log to a third party.

One idea that I think could become the defining feature is this:

Every exhibition should answer the question: “Why these items, and not thousands of others?”

Not by exposing chain-of-thought or long reasoning, but by having a clear curatorial thesis. If an exhibition couldn’t justify its own existence, it probably shouldn’t exist. That gives you a natural quality bar: the AI isn’t rewarded for generating more exhibitions, it’s rewarded for generating exhibitions that feel inevitable once you’ve seen them. I think that’s a much stronger design principle than optimizing for engagement or novelty. It also keeps the project aligned with the museum metaphor rather than drifting into “AI-generated content.”

My answer is:

Don’t make “website” the output. Make it one possible renderer.

Right now you’re thinking:

    ↓
LLM
    ↓

    ↓

    ↓
Exhibition specification

Here’s the architecture I’d personally build

I think there are actually three creative agents, each with very narrow responsibilities:

Archive
        │
        ▼
Indexer
- embeddings
- OCR
- transcripts
- metadata
- scene descriptions
        │
        ▼
Curator
Produces:
- title
- thesis
- selected assets
- ordering
- exhibition notes
        │
        ▼
Exhibition Designer
Produces:
- layout
- typography
- transitions
- interaction
- colors
        │
        ▼
Renderer
HTML
PDF
Video
Slideshow
Print

Notice how each agent is doing something a human equivalent would do. A museum curator doesn’t choose fonts. A graphic designer doesn’t decide which artifacts belong in the exhibition. A printer doesn’t redesign the catalog.

One thought that might shape the project: the output of a curator should be stable enough that, if AI disappeared tomorrow, you could still render every exhibition ever created. That means the lasting artifact is the exhibition specification, not the generated HTML. HTML becomes disposable. The exhibition becomes part of the archive itself. I think that’s a much more durable foundation.
